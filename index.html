The Office of Calculation: From Clerks to Code

I began with Fibonacci, but the real issue is what changes when calculation moves from a staff of human computers to a formal program. This artifact compares those worlds and asks whether programming removes ambiguity and human judgment, or merely hides and redistributes them in another language.

Human Office

Calculation as management, memory, hierarchy, and verification.

Office of Sequential Calculation Instructions for the Production of Fibonacci Numbers The first clerk shall enter the initial two values in the ledger as follows: Term 1 = 0 Term 2 = 1 For each succeeding term, the following division of labor shall be observed.

Clerk A: Keeper of the Previous Term

Retains the older of the two most recent values.

Clerk B: Keeper of the Current Term

Retains the newer of the two most recent values.

Clerk C: Summation Clerk

Adds the values supplied by Clerk A and Clerk B and enters the next term.

Clerk D: Verifying Clerk

Confirms that the new value equals the sum of the previous two values before it advances.

Clerk E: Advancement Clerk

Updates the ledger and restores order after dispute or correction.
Marginal note: In a human office, memory and verification must be assigned to people, not hidden inside variables.

What the Artifact is Doing

The office side makes calculation look like organized labor: roles, memory, hierarchy, verification, and the possibility of misunderstanding. The machine side makes calculation look like formal procedure: variables, loops, registers, and a faster compression of action into rule.

The “When the Rule Changes” interaction introduces a sequence that begins like Fibonacci but shifts after step 5, alternating between ordinary addition and addition mod 7. I use that not as a mathematically pure sequence, but as a model of rule instability or interpretive drift.

The dispute and happy accident controls make visible two things formal code often hides: that correctness can be contested, and that not every deviation is mere failure. Some become novelty.

Comparison + Jones Note

Memo

Makes visible labor, accountability, checking, and social coordination.

Program

Makes visible procedure and automation while concealing much of the office behind syntax.

Dispute

Shows that certainty can be procedural, provisional, or political.

Accident

Shows that deviation may produce error, but may also produce new form.

Final note: programming is not pure, disembodied thought. It is iterative, social, and craft-like work; the shift from memo to code reorganizes judgment rather than abolishing it.
