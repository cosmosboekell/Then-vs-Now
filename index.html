      Reflection

      This artifact keeps Week 3’s question about human computers and formal programs, but moves it into a modern decision system. Instead of computing Fibonacci, it stages a loan decision in two forms: as human committee judgment and as formal scoring. The contrast helped me think about programming not as pure thought, but as a way of translating human judgment into variables, weights, thresholds, and categories.

      The human side makes hesitation, contradiction, and social inference visible. The formal side makes the same decision look cleaner and calmer. But once I added hidden weights, threshold shifts, variable removal, proxy reveals, and a same-applicant comparison, it became harder to believe that the program had eliminated judgment. It had mostly reorganized it. That was the main surprise: code can make a social decision feel neutral without actually removing the assumptions underneath it.

      The point is not that human judgment is messy and code is pure. The point is that programming can make inherited judgment look objective.
