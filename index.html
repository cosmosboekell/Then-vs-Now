NOTE — NESTED PROGRAM
The committee believed it was exercising independent human judgment. But the collateral damage estimate on which three members relied was itself generated by an automated model — CASPAR-7 — whose methodology is classified. The committee was a human layer on top of a program it could not interrogate.
Despite this, the deliberation remains visible. You know who authorized. You know who objected and why. The JAG's legal reasoning can be challenged. Responsibility has a name — several of them.

THE SAME STRIKE  ·  TWO SYSTEMS  ·  ONE QUESTION
Both systems authorized the strike.
What is different between them?
SYSTEM A  ·  COMMITTEE
Is the reasoning visible?
Partially. Each member's stated rationale is on record.
Can the authorization be contested?
Yes. The JAG's proportionality assessment can be challenged. Members can be questioned.
Is bias or error present?
Yes — and some of it is named. The commander's anchoring on 91%. The collateral estimate from a nested program nobody interrogated.
Where is responsibility?
With five named individuals. Uncomfortable. Locatable.
Who can be held accountable?
Each member. The commander. The institution. The government that deployed them.
SYSTEM B  ·  ALGORITHM
Is the reasoning visible?
No. The model's internal weights are classified.
Can the authorization be contested?
Rarely. "The system scored 8.3" is treated as intelligence, not argument. There is no one to cross-examine.
Is bias or error present?
Yes — but unlabeled. If prior targeting was biased, the model learned from that bias. The false positive rate is classified.
Where is responsibility?
Distributed across the programmer, the training data, the institution that certified the model, the human who confirmed the score.
Who can be held accountable?
No one clearly. The programmer only wrote code. The analyst only confirmed a number. The model only identified a pattern.
THE DEEPER PROBLEM — NESTED PROGRAMS
In System A, the committee believed it was the human check on the machine. But its collateral damage estimate came from CASPAR-7. Its pattern-of-life confidence came from the same automated system driving System B. The committee was not a human layer above the program — it was a human layer inside a stack of programs, none of which it could fully see. Even System A's accountability is partially fictional.
The committee makes responsibility visible, contested, and uncomfortable.
The algorithm makes responsibility distributed, hidden, and nearly impossible to locate.

But the deeper problem is that the committee was already downstream of programs it could not interrogate — until the humans inside believed they were deciding, when they were largely confirming.
This artifact is a philosophical argument about the structure of delegated decision-making in automated systems.

A companion artifact, Locus (2026), places the user inside the chain directly and asks them to accept or decline responsibility at each node.
